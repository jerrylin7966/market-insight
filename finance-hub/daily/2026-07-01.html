What's Moving Markets Today

Wednesday, July 1, 2026
By MarketPhase Research
Market Summary
Markets closed Q2 with tech stocks staging a recovery, but the broader tone remains cautiously optimistic as investors confront evidence of economic cooling. Weak June jobs data (98,000 private job additions vs. expectations) and growing AI disruption concerns are creating a bifurcated market where mega-cap AI winners diverge sharply from companies facing structural headwinds. The Dow declined on the jobs miss, while selective strength in semiconductors and cloud infrastructure highlights investor focus on AI-adjacent beneficiaries. Jamie Dimon's candid remarks about the bull market being unsustainable add weight to a growing chorus of warnings that this rally may have gotten ahead of fundamentals.

Key Stories

Jobs Miss Signals Economic Cooling as Q2 Closes

Private employers added just 98,000 jobs in June, materially below expectations and suggesting labor market momentum is decelerating faster than the Fed anticipated. This data point arrives as markets have already priced in rate cuts, but the miss raises questions about whether the economy can sustain current earnings expectations without monetary stimulus. For individual investors, this underscores the tension at the heart of the current rally: either the Fed cuts rates aggressively and stocks are justified, or economic weakness is more pronounced than consensus believes and multiple compression awaits.

AI Disruption Concerns Trigger Selective Sell-Offs Despite Tech Rally

HubSpot (HUBS) was exited by TimesSquare Mid Cap Growth Strategy specifically due to AI disruption risks, while CoStar Group (CSGP) fell despite solid results, reflecting investor anxiety about companies whose business models face structural AI threats. This pattern—strong fundamentals insufficient to support valuations in the face of long-term competitive concerns—signals a meaningful shift in how growth stocks are being evaluated. Investors should recognize that 'beating earnings' no longer guarantees stock appreciation; the market is increasingly pricing in existential threats to traditional software and service models.

Meta, Nvidia Ecosystem Diverge: Cloud AI Capacity Becomes New Battleground

Meta's reported move to monetize excess AI infrastructure capacity and the strong performance of Lattice Semiconductor (LSCC) and Monolithic Power Systems (MPWR) reveal the market's conviction that AI infrastructure spending will remain elevated. These are the picks-and-shovels winners of the AI cycle, and their outperformance relative to end-market software companies illustrates a crucial insight: investors betting on AI should focus on the hardware and infrastructure layer where defensibility and margin expansion are most tangible. Watch for more companies announcing infrastructure-as-a-service offerings—this is where the durable competitive advantages likely reside.

Crypto Extends Weakness as Bitcoin Records Worst Month Since June 2022

Bitcoin's poor performance in June amid broader risk-off sentiment suggests crypto is trading more as a risk asset than a macro hedge. The weakness arrives as traditional markets rally, implying investors are not turning to crypto as an inflation or currency-debasement hedge—they're using it as speculative leverage. For conservative portfolios, this reinforces that crypto remains a high-volatility allocation that should never exceed risk tolerance, regardless of long-term narrative appeal.

Dimon's 'Tsunami' Warning: Bull Market Sustainability Questioned by Banking Insider

JPMorgan's CEO described the current rally as a 'little tsunami' that even he finds surprising, but explicitly stated it will stop, signaling skepticism about valuation levels and sustainability. Coming from one of the financial system's most influential voices, this commentary carries weight—Dimon has insider access to credit conditions, corporate earnings visibility, and capital flows that few others possess. Individual investors should interpret this not as a market-timing signal but as a reminder that valuations have extended significantly and downside risks, while not imminent, are material and asymmetric.

Small-Cap Gains (Casey's +32%, 374Water Russell Entry) Mask Underlying Fragility

While selected small-caps posted impressive quarterly gains and new entrants like 374Water joined the Russell Microcap index, these isolated wins obscure a market environment where many mid-cap and small-cap companies face either AI disruption (CBRE's 'disintermediation' concerns) or slowing growth. The strength in specific pockets does not represent broad-based small-cap outperformance; rather, it reflects continued concentration in names with specific AI or infrastructure tailwinds. Investors considering small-cap exposure should focus on structural industry dynamics rather than recent price performance.

Tech Recovery at Q2 Close Masks Sector Rotation Concerns

While tech stocks recovered into the close of Q2, the underlying drivers—semiconductor and cloud strength—differ markedly from the 'software and SaaS' names that led earlier in the cycle. This intra-sector rotation suggests the all-in bet on mega-cap AI stocks is maturing and investors are now rotating toward more operational/hardware beneficiaries. The fact that recovery is needed at all signals that Q2 saw significant volatility, and complacency about the tech sector's safety would be premature.

Sectors in Focus

Technology and semiconductors are the tale of two stories: infrastructure-layer semiconductors (Lattice, MPS, Sandisk, Micron) are performing strongly as investors lock in AI infrastructure buildout assumptions, while traditional SaaS and software-as-a-service names face existential AI disruption concerns. Discretionary and mid-cap services companies are under pressure (CBRE, Pinterest advertising pullbacks), reflecting both margin concerns and competitive threats. Travel and leisure showed relative strength (buoyed by Iran peace deal geopolitical relief narrative), but this appears tactical rather than thematic. The real leadership remains in companies monetizing AI infrastructure or avoiding software commoditization—a narrow set of winners that is increasingly divorced from broad market advancement.

Macro Note

The June jobs miss arrives at a critical juncture: the Fed has signaled patience with rate cuts, but labor market weakness may force its hand faster than anticipated. June crypto weakness and mixed equity technicals suggest the 'risk-on' sentiment underpinning the bull market may be fraying at the edges, particularly as Q3 begins with seasonal volatility historically higher than Q2. Global factors (Iran peace deal, Swedish court antitrust action against Google) are adding to the macro kaleidoscope, but the dominant narrative remains U.S. AI leadership and the sustainability of elevated profit margins. The tension between economic slowdown signals and equity valuations predicated on continued margin expansion is the macro risk that deserves the most investor attention heading into Q3.

What This Means For You

Today's market action reinforces three critical lessons for long-term investors: first, the current rally is increasingly narrow and concentrated in infrastructure-layer AI beneficiaries, making broad diversification a prudent hedge; second, earnings beats alone are no longer sufficient to support valuations if long-term competitive positioning is threatened by AI (HubSpot, CoStar, CBRE examples), so quality of earnings and defensibility matter more than quarterly beat/miss; third, economic data (the jobs miss) and insider commentary (Dimon) suggest valuations have extended meaningfully, and while this doesn't mean sell everything, it does mean this is an environment to raise cash, take profits on conviction winners, and avoid 'fear of missing out' on highly valued names. The next 4-6 weeks will be critical—watch for how companies guide on margin assumptions and AI-related competitive threats during earnings season. Individual investors should stress-test whether their tech allocations are balanced by operational/dividend businesses that perform well in slower-growth scenarios.

MarketPhase Take

We are witnessing the transition from a 'broad AI enthusiasm' market to a 'AI infrastructure winner-take-most' market, and this transition is highly treacherous for diversified portfolios. The jobs miss, while important, is less significant than the behavioral shift visible in today's headlines: investors are no longer asking 'which companies benefit from AI?' but rather 'which AI investments actually generate cash flow and defensible competitive moats?' This is a healthy recalibration, but it comes at a moment when valuations are extended and breadth is deteriorating. Dimon's warning is not to be dismissed—the 'tsunami' comment reflects genuine concern from someone with the most important vantage point in U.S. finance. We are no longer in the easy phase of the bull market; we are in the phase where selection, discipline, and humility about unknowns matter most. Investors should expect higher volatility, wider dispersion between winners and losers, and periodic tests of resolve.

Market Outlook

The coming week marks the beginning of Q3 with likely elevated seasonal volatility. Watch for earnings guidance on AI margin assumptions and competitive threats (the HubSpot/CBRE pattern may accelerate). The employment report arrives as a key focal point for Fed policy interpretation—another weak print could accelerate rate-cut expectations and stabilize equities, or it could trigger a reassessment of recession risks. Geopolitically, monitor developments on the Iran peace deal (tailwind for energy/defense) and the Google antitrust case implications. Most immediately, track the technical health of the Dow's decline today against the tech recovery—if leadership narrows further, expect choppiness that could test investor conviction in the current rally's sustainability.
