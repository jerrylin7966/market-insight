What's Moving Markets Today

Thursday, July 23, 2026
By MarketPhase Research
Market Summary
U.S. futures are retreating as markets grapple with competing pressures: surging oil prices above $90/barrel, escalating Middle East tensions, and a dawning realization that the AI boom is consuming capital at an unsustainable pace. Big Tech earnings have largely beaten expectations, but massive capital expenditure guidance is spooking investors who worry about near-term profitability being sacrificed for uncertain long-term AI returns. The narrative has shifted from 'AI will save earnings' to 'AI will tank free cash flow,' a meaningful pivot that's creating winners and losers across the board.

Key Stories

Google & Tesla Disappoint on Capex Despite Strong Earnings

Both companies delivered earnings beats, but stock prices fell sharply on guidance: Google's cloud revenue surged 82% yet the stock sold off on elevated capex growth, while Tesla's $25 billion capex plan for coming years spooked investors despite mixed Q2 results. This reveals a critical inflection point—Wall Street no longer assumes AI investments automatically translate to near-term shareholder value. Investors should monitor capex intensity ratios across mega-cap tech; companies that can deliver AI upside without destroying FCF will outperform those caught in an arms race.

IBM Stock Plunges on Earnings Miss; CEO Defends AI Strategy

IBM took a historic stock hit after missing earnings targets, with leadership claiming the company is on the right AI path—a statement that failed to reassure markets. The divergence between management confidence and investor skepticism suggests IBM's AI transformation thesis lacks concrete near-term catalysts or proof points. This is a warning flag for any legacy tech player betting its turnaround on AI; execution risk is real and the market is impatient.

Space Exploration Technologies (SPCX) Loses $1 Trillion in Monthly Selloff

A hypergrowth darling has seen extraordinary valuation compression in just one month, raising the question of whether this is capitulation (a buying opportunity) or a canary in the coal mine for unprofitable growth stocks. The magnitude of the move suggests sentiment is shifting away from 'growth at any cost' narratives. Watch for follow-through selling in other unproven growth names; this could signal that retail and institutional investors are simultaneously de-risking speculative positions.

Oil Prices Rally Above $90 as Geopolitical Risk Escalates

Crude has gained 3% today and broken through the $90 level, driven by widening Middle East conflicts and international demand strengthening (per Halliburton's beat). This is creating a stagflationary backdrop: energy stocks are outperforming (Halliburton topped estimates), but energy inflation is a tax on discretionary consumer spending and corporate margins. Monitor whether this persists; sustained $90+ oil could force the Fed to keep rates higher for longer, complicating the soft-landing narrative.

Semiconductor ETFs Cool After Astronomical Gains

The sector that powered much of the 2024-2026 rally is now rolling over despite Aehr Test rocketing 27.9% on 3x revenue growth outlook, suggesting bifurcation is emerging within semiconductors. Winners are those with concrete near-term growth visibility; losers are those caught in the capex arms race without differentiated demand. This mirrors the broader market's message today: growth is not enough without profitability and capital efficiency.

Bloom Energy Soars 14.8% on Analyst Upgrade Ahead of Earnings

One of the rare big gainers today, Bloom Energy's jump reflects analyst enthusiasm ahead of Q2 results—likely driven by clean energy tailwinds and potential policy support. This suggests pockets of the market are still hunting for growth narratives, but the bar is higher: execution and margin expansion matter as much as revenue. Watch whether this is a sustainable thesis or a fade into earnings.

News Corp Countersues Brave Over AI Content Scraping

A growing legal and regulatory battle is emerging over AI training data usage, with News Corp now actively defending copyright against Brave's scraping. This reflects broader tension between AI companies' insatiable data hunger and content creators' rights—a dynamic that could result in licensing costs, regulatory mandates, or technology friction that pressures AI company margins. Track similar lawsuits and FCA/regulatory moves (e.g., Anthropic joining UK AI Sandbox); this is a structural cost that's not yet priced into AI euphoria.

Sectors in Focus

Energy and oil-related names are the rare outperformers today: Halliburton topped estimates on international demand strength and EQT raised production guidance while lowering capex forecasts—a refreshing combination that pairs growth with capital discipline. Technology is bifurcated and under pressure; mega-cap cloud/AI players are caught between strong fundamentals and excessive capex burn, while semiconductor ETFs are cooling after a multi-year rally. Financial services (Charles Schwab, EFG International) are steady, benefiting from the retail investing boom, but income ETFs are struggling in a higher-rate environment despite tax-friendly fund innovations. Cleantech (Bloom Energy) is finding pockets of support ahead of earnings, but most growth stocks are in retreat as the market demands proof of near-term profitability.

Macro Note

The macro backdrop is shifting from a 'soft landing' narrative toward stagflationary concerns. Oil above $90 and widening geopolitical conflict are real inflation vectors that constrain the Fed's room to cut rates aggressively in the near term. The AI capex boom—while a positive long-term productivity story—is creating a near-term capital efficiency problem that pressures free cash flow and earnings growth, which could slow the economy if sustained. Currency and debt dynamics are likely stressed as rates stay elevated; watch for pressure on emerging markets and leverage-heavy sectors. The confluence of sticky inflation, geopolitical risk, and slowing FCF growth suggests the 'easy money' phase of this cycle is ending.

What This Means For You

Today's market action is a watershed moment: the market is shifting from rewarding pure growth to demanding profitable growth with capital discipline. Individual investors should take three actions: (1) Rebalance exposure to mega-cap tech and unprofitable growth; valuations have not adjusted proportionally to the capex shock and FCF deterioration now evident in earnings guidance. (2) Rotate into energy, utilities, and other sectors benefiting from higher commodity prices and capital discipline (Halliburton, EQT, Bloom Energy on dips offer better risk-reward than mega-cap tech at current valuations). (3) Stress-test your portfolio for a $90+ oil environment sustained for 6-12 months; this is a material headwind to consumer spending and margin expansion that's not fully priced in. Watch individual company capex guidance closely—firms that grow capex less than revenue growth will outperform.

MarketPhase Take

We are witnessing the end of the 'capex don't matter' phase of the AI cycle. For 18 months, the market has given unlimited benefit-of-the-doubt to mega-cap tech companies burning massive amounts of capital on AI infrastructure, betting that future earnings would justify it. Today's selloff in Google and Tesla despite earnings beats signals that patience is exhausted—investors are now pricing in a multi-year period of FCF dilution and lower returns on invested capital. This is the inflection from 'growth at any cost' to 'growth must earn its cost.' Paradoxically, this should benefit disciplined capex stories (energy, select industrials) and create generational buying opportunities in tech names that can decelerate capex growth without sacrificing long-term AI positioning. The cycle is turning, and it will be volatile.

Market Outlook

Key events to watch: (1) Any escalation in Middle East tensions over the next 48-72 hours could push oil above $95 and force broader portfolio de-risking; (2) Retail earnings and guidance (Target, Walmart, etc.) in coming weeks will test whether higher oil/commodity costs are flowing through to consumer prices and denting demand; (3) Fed commentary and PCE inflation data will signal whether the central bank sees stagflationary risk as material; (4) Additional mega-cap tech earnings next week (Amazon, Microsoft, Meta) will test whether the capex shock is uniform or idiosyncratic, and whether the market's newfound skepticism spreads. Position for volatility and selective rotation—this is not a time for complacency.
